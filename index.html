Speed Game

This is a full-stack project that I collaborated on with three other members. The stack that we used for this project is MERN (MongoDB, Express, React, Node.js). The project is a web-based card game that allows two players to play against each other in real-time. The game includes two modes: Traditional and California. The Traditional Speed Game is a fast-paced card game for two players where the goal is to be the first to get rid of all your cards by playing them on center piles based on sequential order, without regard to suit. Players rapidly play cards one higher or one lower than the top cards of the center piles, continuously drawing from their personal decks to maintain a five-card hand, with the game demanding quick reflexes and instant decision-making. California Speed is a variation of the traditional Speed card game, and the objective is similar to the traditional, which is the first one get rid of all cards. But in California Speed, players do not take turns. Instead, both players flip over cards from their piles simultaneously and place them on either of two central piles if the cards match in rank, with no need to match them in sequential order. The game's functionality includes authentication, lobby room where users find a room, waiting room, a top high score board, and chat functionality for players in game and lobby.

Image Processing

In this project, I utilized multiple MPI ranks for image smoothing, leveraging information from neighboring ranks with MPI sends and receives. The image is divided into four strips, for each of the four MPI ranks, with each rank using all available CPU cores for OpenMP and MPI parallelism. The Gaussian Smoothing algorithm is applied, adjusting pixel colors based on nearby pixel values, where closer pixels have more influence. For a detailed explanation of Gaussian Smoothing, see here.

Portable Parallel N-Body With Barnes-Hut

In this project, I leveraged Koko to develop a portable parallel application capable of running the Barnes-Hut algorithm on both CPU and GPU architectures. This approach significantly enhances the efficiency of n-body simulations, which typically present an O(n2) computational complexity. By applying the Barnes-Hut algorithm, the complexity is reduced to O(n log n), dramatically speeding up the process. The fundamental principles of this algorithm are well-documented on the Princeton University course archive, which can be accessed here. For this implementation, the simulation was executed with an N-Body count of 100,000, showcasing the algorithm's capability to efficiently manage large-scale simulations.
